NGS-Based RNA-Seq Analysis

Project Overview

This project investigates differential gene expression across human liver and skin tissues using an NGS-based RNA-Seq workflow. Leveraging transcriptomic data from the Human Protein Atlas, the study aims to uncover tissue-specific gene signatures and biological pathways, particularly those linked to liver-related skin manifestations. The approach integrates computational bioinformatics techniques to analyze high-throughput sequencing data and interpret the molecular basis of tissue-specific health outcomes.

Key Features

  • Data Source: RNA-seq datasets from human liver and skin tissues (Uppsala University Hospital via Human Protein Atlas).
  • Technology Platform: Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS).
  • Analysis Focus:
    • Identification of Differentially Expressed Genes (DEGs).
    • Comparison of metabolic and structural gene expression profiles between tissues.
    • Discovery of gene interactions and biological functions using enrichment and network analyses.
  • Applications:
    • Insights into cutaneous indicators of liver disease (e.g., jaundice, keratin gene shifts).
    • Informing biomarker development and disease diagnostics.

Pipeline Workflow

1. Data Preprocessing

  • Quality Control: FastQC v0.11.9 used to assess sequencing quality.
  • Trimming: Trim Galore removed adapters and low-quality sequences.

2. Alignment & Quantification

  • Mapping: HISAT2 aligned reads to the human reference genome.
  • SAM/BAM Handling: SAMtools used for post-alignment processing.
  • Visualization: IGV (Integrative Genomics Viewer) enabled mapping inspection.

3. Differential Expression Analysis (in RStudio v4.3.1)

  • Tools Used: DESeq2, edgeR, Genefilter, Pheatmap, RColorBrewer
  • Outputs:
    • PCA and MDS plots to separate tissue clusters.
    • Smear plots and heatmaps for DEGs.
    • Statistical identification of up/downregulated genes.

4. Functional Interpretation

  • Enrichment Analysis: DAVID identified Gene Ontology (GO) terms and KEGG pathways.
  • Interaction Network: STRING database revealed gene–gene interactions and pathway clusters.

Conclusion

The study successfully demonstrates a complete NGS-RNA-Seq workflow for transcriptomic comparison between liver and skin tissues. Key findings highlight:

  • The role of early liver-specific gene expression (e.g., CYP, UGT, ALDH families).
  • Skin-tissue-specific upregulation of keratin-related genes (KRT1–KRT14).
  • Biological connections between liver dysfunction and skin symptoms through gene-level evidence.

By integrating high-throughput sequencing and bioinformatics, the project advances our understanding of tissue-specific disease biology and supports precision diagnostics and targeted interventions in clinical genomics.
